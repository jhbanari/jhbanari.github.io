02  ·  Process

How the research
came together

This project unfolded over two academic quarters and moved through five overlapping stages — from the conceptual to the empirical to the interpretive. Here is how it developed.

I

Preparation — Situating the Question

I began by identifying the gap: after the FDA's 2023 approval of Opill as the first OTC oral contraceptive, almost no research centered college-aged people as a distinct population. I conducted an extensive literature review drawing on clinical research, reproductive justice scholarship (Ross & Solinger), feminist epistemology (Haraway, Martin), and health policy analysis (Grossman & Grindlay, Wynne et al.) to build the theoretical framework that would anchor the study.

II

Design — Choosing a Mixed-Methods Approach

I chose a mixed-methods, cross-sectional design because the research questions required both breadth and depth — statistical patterns to map the distribution of attitudes across demographic groups, and qualitative accounts to explain the mechanisms behind those patterns. The study population was UC Davis undergraduates who were biologically capable of pregnancy, recruited through the SONA research participation platform.

III

Activities — Data Collection

I designed and administered an anonymous online survey through Qualtrics. The instrument combined Likert-scale measures (awareness, knowledge confidence, safety concern, likelihood of use) with embedded and closing open-ended prompts that invited participants to reflect on their experiences, concerns, and social contexts. 56 undergraduates completed the full survey. IRB protocols ensured anonymity and participant protection throughout.

IV

Synthesis — Analysis and Integration

Quantitative data were analyzed using descriptive statistics; qualitative responses were analyzed using inductive thematic analysis following Braun and Clarke (2006). Integration occurred at the interpretive stage: I examined where quantitative patterns and qualitative themes converged, diverged, and complicated each other. Five major themes emerged — safety concerns, cost and insurance gaps, stigma, informational deficits, and informal knowledge networks.

V

Presentation & Impact — Writing the Thesis

The final thesis situates empirical findings within the reproductive justice and feminist healthcare literatures to argue that OTC availability is a meaningful but insufficient advance — its equity effects are contingent on structural conditions that the regulatory change alone does not supply. The thesis advances a formal hypothesis for future research and outlines policy implications for insurance reform, public health education, and culturally responsive outreach.

03  ·  Product

The thesis &
key findings

Honors Thesis · UC Davis · 2026

Shaping Access and Autonomy: Determinants of College Students' Awareness and Attitudes Towards Over-the-Counter Birth Control

This mixed-methods study examines what shapes undergraduate students' awareness of and attitudes toward Opill — the FDA-approved OTC oral contraceptive — and how those factors influence their willingness to use it. Using survey data from 56 UC Davis undergraduates, the study finds that formal availability does not automatically translate into confident, informed, or economically accessible use. Awareness, confidence, and willingness are patterned by structural conditions: uneven reproductive health education, insurance navigation barriers, cultural stigma, and racialized institutional trust.

04  ·  Reflection

Looking back,
looking forward

What would I change about the process?

The sample, while rich in qualitative texture, was limited to UC Davis undergraduates — a relatively resource-advantaged group. If I were doing this again, I would build in recruitment infrastructure from the start to reach community college students and young adults not enrolled in four-year universities, the populations most burdened by the structural barriers OTC approval was meant to address. I would also collect racial and ethnic identity through fixed categories alongside open-text entry, which would have allowed for more rigorous quantitative subgroup analysis without sacrificing the nuance of self-identification.

What would I keep the same?

The mixed-methods design was the right call, and I would not change it. The decision to wait until the interpretive stage to integrate quantitative and qualitative findings — rather than trying to force them together earlier — gave each method room to breathe and produced triangulation that felt genuinely earned rather than procedural. I would also keep the theoretical framework. Grounding the study in reproductive justice scholarship and feminist epistemology gave the findings analytic depth that a purely descriptive study would not have had.

Would I recommend this kind of project to another student?

Absolutely — and specifically to students who have something they genuinely want to know. The hardest part of the thesis was not the mechanics of data collection or the writing; it was tolerating the uncertainty of not knowing what the data would say. But that uncertainty is also where the intellectual aliveness is. If you care about the question enough, the process of not-yet-knowing is bearable. And the answer you arrive at feels like it belongs to you in a way that a literature review alone never does.

How has this project shaped my professional and academic development?

This project taught me that I can hold complexity without collapsing it. Working across quantitative patterns and qualitative themes, across clinical literature and feminist theory, across what students said and what the data showed — that demanded a kind of intellectual patience I did not know I had. It also deepened my conviction that equity work requires structural analysis, not just compassion. The findings of this study are findings I intend to carry into my professional life, wherever it leads next.
